Instead of a direct comparison `a > b`, the strategy should be to prove that `a` is meaningfully larger than `b` by a margin that exceeds potential representation errors. The correct approach is to show that the difference `a - b` is greater than a small, positive tolerance (epsilon), thus proving `a - b > f64::EPSILON`.

**Concrete Fixes:**

- Calculate the full kinetic energy for each object to make the comparison explicit: `let ke1 = 0.5 * m1 * v1.powi(2);` and `let ke2 = 0.5 * m2 * v2.powi(2);`.
- Replace the fragile direct comparison `term1 > term2` with a robust epsilon comparison to handle floating-point inaccuracies near equality: `(ke1 - ke2) > std::f64::EPSILON`.

**Structural Notes:** Decompose the logic by creating a separate, private helper function `fn calculate_ke(m: f64, v: f64) -> f64`. The main function `has_greater_kinetic_energy` will then call this helper for both objects and perform the robust epsilon comparison on the results. This isolates the physics calculation from the numerical comparison logic, improving modularity, testability, and clarity.
